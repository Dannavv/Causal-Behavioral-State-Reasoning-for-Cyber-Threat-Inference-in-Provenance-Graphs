\chapter{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{ch:evaluation}

This chapter presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation of CBSR across five diverse
benchmarks under clean, contaminated, and concept-drift conditions. Dataset statistics,
hardware environment, and evaluation protocol are detailed in Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation}.
Baselines include Isolation Forest, LOF, One-Class SVM, and PyTorch Autoencoder --- all trained
on the same first-50\% chronological partition as CBSR --- plus the pure causal v1 baseline.

\textbf{Key findings:}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{OpTC}: CBSR achieves AUC of 0.9628---a $+12.7\%$ improvement over the pure causal
    baseline (0.8359), surpassing both Isolation Forest (0.5968) and Autoencoder (0.9364). At
    95\% recall, CBSR yields an operational FPR of \textbf{10.14\%}---a 4$\times$ reduction over
    the pure causal baseline (44.57\%).
\item \textbf{BETH}: AUC of 0.9901, the highest among all methods, demonstrating cross-platform
    generalization from Windows provenance to Linux sysdig telemetry.
\item \textbf{TC3, NODLINK}: Strong AUC of 0.9837 and 0.9784, significantly improving the pure
    causal baseline (0.835, 0.826) by $+$14.9\% and $+$15.2\% respectively.
\item \textbf{StreamSpot}: AUC improves from 0.4991 (random guessing) to 0.6909 by combining
    temporal sequence reasoning ($R_T$) and behavioral manifold classification ($R_B$). The
    residual weakness is analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:streamspot}.
\end{itemize}

\section{StreamSpot: Analysis of Residual Weakness}
\label{sec:streamspot}

StreamSpot is our most challenging benchmark (AUC 0.6909). The dataset contains graphs from five
heterogeneous workload types (YouTube, Gmail, VGame, Download, CNN). A \emph{single} linear SCM
learned across all workload types is a poor fit for any individual type, producing systematically
inflated residuals that desensitize the Mechanism Violation coordinates ($s_8$--$s_{11}$). The
improvement from 0.4991 to 0.6909 comes primarily from $R_T$ recognizing that an active
drive-by download sequence differs from normal workload transitions. Two architectural improvements
would significantly raise this: (1) workload-type conditioning via graph clustering pre-pass, and
(2) mixture-of-experts SCMs with soft workload assignment---both targeted in future work.

\section{Concept Drift Adaptation}
\label{sec:drift}

We simulate a benign concept drift event on TC3 by doubling the event rate of the five most
active normal edges at test step 200. Pre-drift, all methods target a 5\% FPR budget. After
drift onset:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Isolation Forest}: FPR surges to 30.0\%
\item \textbf{Autoencoder}: FPR surges to 51.5\%
\item \textbf{CBSR}: FPR recovers to \textbf{9.2\%} after online SCM refit ($3.2\times$ lower
    than IF, $5.6\times$ lower than AE)
\end{itemize}

CBSR's change-point detector fires at steps 201 and 256, triggering online SCM refits and
conformal queue resets, holding post-drift false alarms far below static alternatives.

\section{Conformal FPR Control}

Under normal streaming, online threshold adaptation maintains empirical false alarm rates
at \textbf{1.78\%--6.13\%} across all five datasets at a 5\% FPR budget. This demonstrates
that conformal calibration successfully decouples detection sensitivity from ad-hoc threshold
tuning and provides a practical operational knob for SOC deployment.
